LeetCode #174

Dungeon Game

Find the minimum initial health the knight needs to rescue the princess — moving only right or down through a grid of rooms that drain or restore health.

Hard Dynamic Programming Reverse DP from End Time: O(m·n) Space: O(m·n)
1 Understanding the Problem ▾

A knight starts at the top-left of a dungeon grid. The princess is trapped at the bottom-right. Each room has an integer value — negative means the room drains health (demons!), positive means it restores health (magic orbs). The knight can only move right or down at each step. The knight dies the instant his health drops to 0 or below. What is the minimum initial health he needs?

⚡ The Trap: Why Forward DP Fails

Your first instinct might be: "track maximum health at each cell — take the path where you arrive healthiest." But this fails. A path through a +100 bonus room might lead into a -200 corridor that kills you, while a lower-health path avoids it. Future cells determine whether your current health is "enough." You cannot optimize locally without knowing the future.

Worked Example (3×3):

Dungeon grid (value = health change when entering room):
  [  -2,  -3,   3  ]   ← row 0
  [  -5, -10,   1  ]   ← row 1
  [  10,  30,  -5  ]   ← row 2 (princess at [2][2])

Question: How much HP does the knight need at [0][0]?
Answer: 7 (we'll verify this step-by-step in Section 6)
🔑 What We're Really Asking

For every cell (i, j): "If the knight enters this cell, what is the minimum health he needs at entry to guarantee he can reach the princess alive?" We call this dp[i][j]. The answer is dp[0][0].

Edge cases: 1×1 grid (knight is at princess cell — needs enough to survive that room, minimum 1), all-positive dungeon (answer is always 1), princess cell itself negative (knight must enter with at least 1 - dungeon[m-1][n-1] HP).

2 The Algorithm: Reverse DP from the Princess ▾

We work backwards — start at the princess cell and fill the DP table toward the knight's start. At each cell, we ask: "what minimum health do I need entering here, given what I know about cells ahead?"

🧠 Why Is This Dynamic Programming?

Optimal Substructure: The minimum HP needed at cell (i, j) depends only on dp[i+1][j] (going down) and dp[i][j+1] (going right). Nothing else from elsewhere in the grid matters. This is a clean recursive sub-problem decomposition.

Overlapping Subproblems: Cell (1,1) can be reached via (0,1)→(1,1) or (1,0)→(1,1). Without memoization, a recursive approach would recompute dp[1][1] once per path — exponentially wasteful for large grids.

📐 The Recurrence Relations
Base case: dp[m-1][n-1] = max(1, 1 − dungeon[m-1][n-1])
Last row (j from n-2 to 0): dp[m-1][j] = max(1, dp[m-1][j+1] − dungeon[m-1][j])
Last column (i from m-2 to 0): dp[i][n-1] = max(1, dp[i+1][n-1] − dungeon[i][n-1])
Interior (i from m-2 to 0, j from n-2 to 0): best = min(dp[i+1][j], dp[i][j+1]) dp[i][j] = max(1, best − dungeon[i][j])

Reading the recurrence: best - dungeon[i][j] computes the required entry HP. If dungeon[i][j] is positive (heals), you need less HP to enter. If negative (drains), you need more. The max(1, ...) clamp ensures the knight is always alive (≥1 HP).

1 Base case — princess cell (m-1, n-1): Knight must survive this cell with ≥1 HP. dp[m-1][n-1] = max(1, 1 - dungeon[m-1][n-1]).
2 Fill last row (right → left): Only exit is rightward. dp[m-1][j] = max(1, dp[m-1][j+1] - dungeon[m-1][j]).
3 Fill last column (bottom → top): Only exit is downward. dp[i][n-1] = max(1, dp[i+1][n-1] - dungeon[i][n-1]).
4 Fill interior cells (bottom-right → top-left): Two exits. Take the cheaper one (min), then apply the recurrence.
5 Return dp[0][0] — the minimum health the knight needs to start.
3 Why It Works: The Invariant ▾
🔐 The Invariant

After we compute dp[i][j], the following holds: "If the knight enters cell (i, j) with exactly dp[i][j] health points, he can reach the princess alive via some path — and dp[i][j] is the minimum such value."

Why min(down, right)? The knight chooses the optimal future path. We want the minimum HP required to enter (i, j). If we use min(dp[i+1][j], dp[i][j+1]), we're asking: "of the two available exits, which requires less HP? Plan for that one." Taking max would over-estimate by pessimistically assuming the worse exit.

Why max(1, ...)? If a room has a large positive value (say +100), the formula gives 1 - 100 = -99. But the knight must have at least 1 HP to be alive. The clamp to 1 reflects this physical constraint — you must be alive to enter any room.

⚠️ Common Mistake: Forward DP with "Max Health Tracking"

Some try dp[i][j] = max(dungeon[i][j] + max(dp[i-1][j], dp[i][j-1]), 1). This tracks the most health you can arrive at each cell. But this doesn't work — a path with maximum health at cell (i,j) might still die in later cells. We care about the survival constraint end-to-end, not the health at any single intermediate cell.

💡 Analogy: Backwards Expedition Planning

Imagine you're planning a dangerous mountain expedition. Rather than guessing how much food to pack from base camp, you work backwards: "How much food do I need at the summit to safely descend? Then how much at the midpoint to reach the summit? Then how much at base camp to reach the midpoint?" Each backward step is a clean, certain calculation — because you know what's needed next.

1×1 edge case: dp[0][0] = max(1, 1 - dungeon[0][0]). If the princess room is -5, the knight needs 6 HP. If it's +10, the knight still needs at least 1 HP — you can't have zero health and be alive.

4 The Code (Annotated) ▾

Solution 1 — Bottom-Up Tabulation (Optimal · Interview Pick)

def calculateMinimumHP(dungeon: list[list[int]]) -> int:
m, n = len(dungeon), len(dungeon[0]) ← grid height and width
dp = [[0] * n for _ in range(m)] ← dp[i][j] = min HP to enter (i,j) and survive
dp[m-1][n-1] = max(1, 1 - dungeon[m-1][n-1]) ← base case: princess cell
for j in range(n-2, -1, -1): ← fill last row right-to-left (only exit: right)
dp[m-1][j] = max(1, dp[m-1][j+1] - dungeon[m-1][j])
for i in range(m-2, -1, -1): ← fill last column bottom-to-top (only exit: down)
dp[i][n-1] = max(1, dp[i+1][n-1] - dungeon[i][n-1])
for i in range(m-2, -1, -1): ← fill interior: bottom-to-top
for j in range(n-2, -1, -1): ← inner loop: right-to-left
best = min(dp[i+1][j], dp[i][j+1]) ← cheaper exit (down vs right)
dp[i][j] = max(1, best - dungeon[i][j])← min HP to enter this cell; clamp at 1
return dp[0][0] ← answer: min HP for knight at start
⚠️ Why max(1, ...) and NOT just the raw formula?

If a room gives +100 HP and the next cell only needs 5 HP, the raw formula gives 5 - 100 = -95. But you can't start with -95 HP — you'd be dead. The clamp enforces the invariant that health is always a positive integer. Even in a room that heals massively, you need at least 1 HP to enter.

Solution 2 — Top-Down Memoization (easier to derive, same complexity)

from functools import lru_cache
def calculateMinimumHP(dungeon: list[list[int]]) -> int:
m, n = len(dungeon), len(dungeon[0])
@lru_cache(maxsize=None)
def dp(i, j) -> int: ← min HP to enter (i,j) and survive
if i == m-1 and j == n-1: ← base case: princess cell
return max(1, 1 - dungeon[i][j])
exits = []
if i + 1 < m: exits.append(dp(i+1, j)) ← can go down
if j + 1 < n: exits.append(dp(i, j+1)) ← can go right
return max(1, min(exits) - dungeon[i][j]) ← pick cheaper exit, apply recurrence
return dp(0, 0) ← start from top-left
💡 Space Optimization: O(n) Rolling Array

We only ever look at the row below and the cell to the right. We can reduce space to O(n) by keeping just one row and updating it in-place right-to-left. Add dp[m] = [float('inf')] * n and dp[m][n-1] = 1 as sentinels, then process each row updating left from right.

5 🎤 Interview Strategy: How to Arrive at This ▾
Step 1: Recognize Grid DP Signal

Grid + right/down moves + optimization → 2D DP. The twist: it's not a simple min/max path sum. There's a survival constraint. Say: "I see grid DP, but we need to track survival, not just total cost. Let me think about what state captures this."

Step 2: Show Forward DP Breaks

Try forward DP mentally: "max health at each cell?" Quickly realize a path with high health at (1,1) might still die in row 2, while a lower-health path avoids deadly cells. Verbalize: "Forward DP can't encode the survival constraint — we need to know future cells to make optimal local decisions."

Step 3: Redefine State → Reverse DP

Say: "Let dp[i][j] = minimum health needed at entry to (i,j) to survive to the end. This is computable backwards — at the princess cell, we know exactly what's needed. Each backward step only depends on cells already computed." This is the insight that unlocks everything.

Step 4: Write the Recurrence Live

"At any interior cell, I can go right or down. I take the cheaper exit (min). Then I need enough HP to enter this cell and still have that much: dp[i][j] = max(1, min(dp[i+1][j], dp[i][j+1]) - dungeon[i][j]). The max(1,...) clamp ensures health stays positive."

🗣️ What to Say to the Interviewer

"I'll use reverse DP. Forward DP doesn't work here because knowing max health at a cell doesn't tell you about future survival.
State: dp[i][j] = minimum HP to enter (i,j) and reach the princess alive.
Base case: princess cell → max(1, 1 - dungeon[m-1][n-1]).
Recurrence: dp[i][j] = max(1, min(dp[i+1][j], dp[i][j+1]) - dungeon[i][j]).
I take min of two exits because the knight picks the optimal path. I clamp at 1 because HP must be positive.
Fill order: bottom-right to top-left. Time O(m·n), Space O(m·n) — reducible to O(n)."
🔮 Likely Follow-up Questions
  • "Can you optimize space?" → Yes, O(n) with a rolling 1D array. Update right-to-left in a single row.
  • "What if movement includes left/up?" → No longer a DAG. Need Dijkstra on a (row, col, health) state graph.
  • "What if you want to maximize final health instead?" → Now forward DP works (no survival constraint mid-path).
  • "What if there are k magic potions to pick up on the path?" → Add dimension: dp[i][j][k_used].

7 Summary & Key Takeaways ▾
Reverse DP from end — forward DP can't satisfy survival constraints without knowing the future
State: dp[i][j] = minimum HP needed entering cell (i,j) to survive to the princess
Recurrence: dp[i][j] = max(1, min(dp[i+1][j], dp[i][j+1]) - dungeon[i][j])
Always clamp to 1 — the knight must be alive (≥1 HP) to enter any room
Take min of exits because the knight will choose the optimal future path
Space optimizable to O(n) with a rolling 1D array updated right-to-left
ApproachTimeSpaceNotes
Brute Force DFS O(2^(m+n)) O(m+n) Explores all paths — exponential
Forward DP INCORRECT — Cannot capture survival constraint
Reverse Tabulation DP ✓ O(m·n) O(m·n) Optimal; interview pick
Memoization (top-down) O(m·n) O(m·n)+stack Easier to derive recursively
Space-optimized DP O(m·n) O(n) Rolling 1D array; bonus points
🎯 When to Use Reverse DP from End
  • Future cells determine whether your current state is "good enough"
  • Forward DP creates ambiguity — local optimality doesn't imply global optimality
  • The terminal condition is well-defined and simple (princess cell here)
  • Path optimization where a constraint must hold throughout the path, not just at the end
Minimum Path SumMedium — Same grid DP; minimize total cost top-left → bottom-right. Forward DP works here — no survival constraint.
