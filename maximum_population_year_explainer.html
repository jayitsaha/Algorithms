LeetCode #1854

Maximum Population Year

Given a list of people's birth and death years, find the earliest year with the most people alive simultaneously.

Easy Prefix Sum Difference Array Time: O(n + R) Space: O(R)
1 Understanding the Problem ▾

You are given a 2D array logs where logs[i] = [birth_i, death_i]. Each person was alive during years [birth_i, death_i - 1] inclusive — they are born in birth_i but are not counted alive in the year they died (death_i). All years fall in the range [1950, 2050].

The goal: return the earliest year in which the maximum number of people were simultaneously alive. If multiple years share the maximum, return the smallest (earliest).

💡 Key Insight

Each person adds 1 to a range of years. We want the point where those ranges overlap most. This is the classic "maximum overlap of intervals" pattern, elegantly solved with a Difference Array: mark +1 at each birth and -1 at each death, then a prefix sum gives the population at every year in O(n + R) total time.

Concrete Example: logs = [[1993,1999],[2000,2010],[1995,2005],[1960,2000],[1975,1985]]. Person 3 overlaps with Person 1 (1995–1998) and Person 4 (1995–1999). In 1995, all three are alive simultaneously, giving population 3 — the maximum. So the answer is 1995.

Edge Cases: All people alive same year (full overlap), no overlap at all, single person (trivially their birth year), all with same birth year, death at 2050 (max boundary).

2 The Algorithm: Difference Array + Prefix Sum ▾

The Difference Array technique is a sub-pattern of Prefix Sum. Instead of incrementing every index in a range [l, r] (O(r−l) per operation), we encode each range update as just two point writes: +1 at the start, -1 at one past the end. A single prefix-sum pass then recovers the true value at every index.

1 Allocate diff[0..100] — 101 slots represent years 1950 to 2050 (index = year − 1950). Initialize all zeros.
2 Record events for each person — For person with birth b and death d: diff[b-1950] += 1 (enters population) and diff[d-1950] -= 1 (leaves population at death year, so NOT counted there).
3 Prefix sum scan — Walk through diff[0..100], accumulate a running population sum. At each index, population equals the number of people alive in year 1950 + i.
4 Track maximum with strict > — If population > max_pop, update max and record best_year = 1950 + i. Strict greater-than (not ≥) ensures ties keep the earlier (smaller) year.

🔑 Why Not Just Brute Force?

Brute force: for each of R=101 years, scan all n persons → O(n × R). With n people and a large year range R, that's expensive. Difference Array: O(n) to write events + O(R) to scan = O(n + R). For arbitrary-range problems (e.g., years 1 to 1,000,000), this advantage becomes dramatic.

3 Why It Works: The Invariant ▾
🔐 The Invariant

After writing all birth/death events, diff[i] equals (number of people born in year 1950+i) minus (number of people who die in year 1950+i). The prefix sum at index i — i.e., sum(diff[0..i]) — equals the number of people who were born on or before year 1950+i and whose death year is strictly greater than 1950+i. That is exactly the count of people alive in year 1950+i.

Proof sketch: For any person with birth b and death d, they contribute +1 to diff at index b-1950 and -1 at index d-1950. In the prefix sum from index 0 to any index i:

  • If i < b-1950: neither +1 nor -1 has been included yet. Net = 0. (Not yet born.)
  • If b-1950 ≤ i < d-1950: the +1 is included but not the -1. Net = +1. (Alive!)
  • If i ≥ d-1950: both +1 and -1 are included. Net = 0. (Dead.)

Summing over all persons at any index i gives exactly the count of currently alive people. The prefix sum correctly separates "born but not yet dead" from "dead" or "not yet born."

⚠️ The Most Common Mistake: Death Year Off-By-One

The person is alive during [birth, death−1]. So the -1 event must be placed at index death − 1950 (the death year itself), NOT at death − 1 − 1950. If you place it one too early, you subtract the person from their last living year, undercounting population. If you place it one too late, you count them alive in their death year, overcounting.

Why strict > preserves the earliest year: If year 1975 gives max_pop=3 and year 1993 also gives 3, 3 > 3 is false, so we never update best_year to 1993. The first year that achieves the maximum is locked in and never displaced by a tie.

4 The Code (Annotated) ▾

Solution 1 — Difference Array (Optimal) · Interview Pick

def maximumPopulation(logs: list[list[int]]) -> int:
diff = [0] * 101 ← Slots 0–100 represent years 1950–2050; birth/death go here
for birth, death in logs: ← Unpack each person's birth and death year from logs
diff[birth - 1950] += 1 ← Person enters population at their birth year
diff[death - 1950] -= 1 ← Person exits at death year (NOT alive in that year)
population = 0 ← Running prefix sum = people alive right now
max_pop = 0 ← Track maximum population seen so far
best_year = 1950 ← Track earliest year with max population
for i in range(101): ← Scan all 101 years (1950 to 2050)
population += diff[i] ← Apply net change: births minus deaths in year 1950+i
if population > max_pop: ← Strict >: ties don't update → preserves earliest year
max_pop = population ← New max population found
best_year = 1950 + i ← Convert index back to actual year
return best_year ← Earliest year where population was at its peak
⚠️ Why strict > and not >=?

The problem asks for the earliest year. Using >= would overwrite best_year every time population equals the current maximum — meaning a later year with the same count would replace an earlier one. We only want to update when we find a strictly higher count, locking in the first (smallest) year where that count occurs.

Solution 2 — Brute Force O(n × R) — state this first in an interview

💡 Complexity Summary

Brute Force: Time O(n × R), Space O(1). R = 101 years here, acceptable for small input.
Difference Array: Time O(n + R), Space O(R). The event-writing pass is O(n); the prefix-sum scan is O(R). For large R (e.g., years in range [1, 10^9] after coordinate compression), this scales far better.
Sorting Events: Time O(n log n), Space O(n). Collect (year, +1/-1) tuples, sort, scan. Generalizes to arbitrary ranges without coordinate compression.

5 🎤 Interview Strategy: How to Arrive at This ▾
Step 1: Identify the "Range Overlap" Signal

The phrase "alive during [birth, death-1]" immediately signals a range problem. Each person contributes to multiple years. Asking "max simultaneous count" is "max overlap of intervals." Two clean approaches: (a) sweep line / difference array, or (b) sort-and-scan events.

Step 2: Start with Brute Force

"For each of the 101 years, count how many persons satisfy birth ≤ year < death. O(n × R) — totally fine for R=101, but let me show the better approach that scales."

Step 3: Propose Difference Array

"Instead of checking every year against every person, I record just two events per person: +1 at birth, -1 at death. A single pass with a running sum gives population at every year. That's O(n + R) — linear in both people and year range."

Step 4: Handle Off-By-One Explicitly

"Important: the -1 goes at the death year itself, not at death-1. Because the person is NOT alive in their death year — that's when the count drops. So diff[death - 1950] -= 1."

🗣️ What to Say to the Interviewer

"Each person is alive during [birth, death-1]. I need the maximum simultaneous overlap of these intervals.
Brute force: for each of 101 years, count alive persons — O(n × 101), fine but not elegant.
Better: difference array. Mark +1 at birth, -1 at death. Prefix sum → population per year.
Scan once, track max with strict greater-than to preserve the earliest year on ties.
Time: O(n + 101) = O(n). Space: O(101) = O(1). Scales to any year range with coord compression."
🔮 Likely Follow-up Questions
  • "Years up to 10^9 instead of 2050?" → Coordinate compress to a 0-indexed array, same technique.
  • "Return all years with max population?" → Two-pass: first find max_pop, then collect all years where running sum equals it.
  • "Maximum at any point in time (not just integer years)?" → Sort events by time, walk with counter — same idea, handles fractional times too.
  • "What's the minimum number of 'rooms' needed for max overlap?" → Same problem (Meeting Rooms II). Difference array gives the answer directly.

7 Summary & Key Takeaways ▾
Difference Array = mark +1 at range start, -1 at range end+1; prefix sum recovers true values
Death year placement: -1 at death index (NOT death-1) — person not alive in death year
Strict > when updating max preserves the earliest year in case of ties
Array offset: subtract YEAR_OFFSET (1950) to map years to indices 0–100
O(n + R) beats O(n × R) brute force; scales to arbitrarily large year ranges

🎯 When to Recognize the Difference Array Pattern
  • "Add k to every index in range [l, r], then read all values" → Difference array
  • "How many intervals overlap at any point?" → Difference array or sort events
  • "Find time/position with maximum simultaneous events" → Mark +1 start, -1 end, prefix sum
  • Bounded integer range (e.g., years 1950–2050, seats 0–999) → fixed array, no need for coord compress
  • Unbounded or very large integer range → coordinate compress first, same technique
