LeetCode #11

Container With Most Water

Find two vertical lines in an array of heights that, together with the x-axis, form a container holding the maximum amount of water.

Medium Two Pointers Opposite Direction · Greedy Time: O(n) Space: O(1)
1 Understanding the Problem ▾

Picture a row of vertical walls standing on a flat floor. You pick any two walls. Water poured between them fills up to the level of the shorter wall — then spills over. The water volume equals the shorter wall's height multiplied by the horizontal distance between them. Your task: find the pair that holds the most water.

💡 Key Insight

Area between walls at left and right = min(height[left], height[right]) × (right − left). Two competing forces: a wider container holds more water, but a shorter wall acts as a bottleneck that limits the height. Finding the maximum requires balancing both.

For height = [1, 8, 6, 2, 5, 4, 8, 3, 7]: walls at index 1 (height 8) and index 8 (height 7) give area = min(8,7) × (8−1) = 7 × 7 = 49. This is the answer.

✗ Naive: Pick the Two Tallest Walls

Tallest walls: index 1 (h=8) and index 6 (h=8).
Area = min(8,8) × (6−1) = 8 × 5 = 40.
Not the maximum! Narrow width hurts.

✓ Correct: Balance Width and Height

Walls: index 1 (h=8) and index 8 (h=7).
Area = min(8,7) × (8−1) = 7 × 7 = 49.
Wider container beats taller but narrower one.

Edge Cases: n=2 — only one pair; answer is always that pair. All equal heights — any pair with maximum width wins. Monotonically decreasing heights — the outermost pair is always optimal (maximum width and the left wall is never the bottleneck for long).

2 The Algorithm: Converging Two-Pointer (Greedy Shrink) ▾

Start with the widest possible container — pointers at both ends. At every step, record the current area, then move the pointer at the shorter wall inward. Repeat until the pointers meet.

1 Initialize: left = 0, right = n − 1, max_area = 0. The widest container gives the best chance before any height tradeoffs.
2 Compute area: area = min(height[left], height[right]) × (right − left). Update max_area if larger.
3 Greedy move — the critical rule: If height[left] < height[right], increment left. Otherwise decrement right. Always move the pointer at the shorter wall.
4 Repeat until left ≥ right. Every pair has now been considered or safely eliminated.
5 Return max_area.
🔐 The Greedy Argument — Why Move the Shorter Pointer?

Suppose height[left] < height[right]. What if we move right inward instead? The width shrinks by 1, but the bottleneck (left) stays the same or gets worse — so area can only decrease. We are guaranteed not to lose the optimal answer by skipping those pairs; they are all ≤ the current area.

3 Why It Works: The Invariant ▾
🔐 The Invariant

At every step, the optimal answer must exist within the current [left, right] window. Every pair outside the window has been provably shown to be suboptimal and is safely eliminated.

Proof sketch (when we move left): Assume height[left] ≤ height[right]. We are about to eliminate all pairs (left, x) for x < right. For any such pair: area = min(height[left], height[x]) × (x − left) ≤ height[left] × (right − left) = the area we just computed (since min(...) ≤ height[left] and x − left < right − left). So all those pairs are at most as good as what we already recorded — safely skipped.

By symmetry, moving right when height[right] ≤ height[left] eliminates all pairs (x, right) for x > left that are at most as good as the current area. The algorithm exhausts all meaningful pairs in one linear pass.

💡 Analogy: Adjustable Plank on a Seesaw

Two people stand at opposite ends of a field, each holding one end of a horizontal plank over their head. The plank's height above the ground is limited by the shorter person. To raise the plank higher, you tell the shorter person to step inward (hoping to find a taller replacement). Asking the taller person to step in reduces the span of the plank without fixing the height constraint — a pure loss.

Equal heights: When height[left] = height[right], you can move either pointer — the skipped pairs are all bounded by the current area. The code moves right in the else-branch by convention; both choices are correct.

Solution 2 — Two-Pointer O(n) — Interview Pick

def maxArea(height): Takes the list of wall heights
left, right = 0, len(height) - 1 Widest span: left at start, right at end
max_area = 0 Track the best area seen so far
while left < right: Loop until pointers meet (no container remains)
h = min(height[left], height[right]) Shorter wall is the height bottleneck
w = right - left Horizontal distance = width of container
max_area = max(max_area, h * w) Update maximum if this container is better
if height[left] < height[right]: Left wall is shorter — it is the bottleneck
left += 1 Move left inward; skipped pairs are all suboptimal
else: Right wall is shorter OR equal (either is fine)
right -= 1 Move right inward; same skipping argument applies
return max_area All pairs considered or eliminated — optimal answer
⚠️ Common Mistake: Moving the Wrong Pointer

Do not always move left, always move right, or move toward the nearest taller wall. The rule is: move the pointer at the shorter (or equal) wall. Moving the taller-wall pointer cannot increase area — it reduces width while keeping the same bottleneck height.

5 🎤 Interview Strategy: How to Arrive at This ▾
Step 1: Recognize the Pattern Signals

Three signals: (1) We need an optimal pair. (2) The objective depends on both the values at the pair AND the distance between them. (3) There's a natural ordering (indices are already in sequence). These are the hallmarks of the opposite-direction two-pointer pattern.

Step 2: State the Brute Force First

"I can try every pair in O(n²) — n up to 10⁵ means n² ≈ 10¹⁰, which is too slow. I want O(n)." This shows you understand constraints and motivates the next optimization.

Step 3: Make the Key Observation

"Start at the widest container. To make it larger, I'd need a taller wall to compensate for losing width. The short wall is the limiting factor — moving it is the only action that could possibly reveal a taller wall and improve the area. Moving the taller wall is provably suboptimal."

Step 4: Prove Correctness (briefly)

"When I move the shorter pointer, I skip pairs (shorter-ptr, everything inside). All those pairs use the same short wall as the height limit and have smaller width — so they're all ≤ the current area. No optimal pair is skipped."

Step 5: State Complexity

"Each pointer advances one step per iteration. Together they cross n total steps. O(n) time, O(1) space."

🗣️ What to Say to the Interviewer

"I'll place two pointers at opposite ends — widest container first. Each step: compute area, update maximum. Then move the pointer at the shorter wall inward — it's the bottleneck, and it's the only move that can improve area. Moving the taller-wall pointer keeps the same bottleneck with less width, which is provably suboptimal. This gives O(n) time and O(1) space."

7 Summary & Key Takeaways ▾
Area = min(height[left], height[right]) × (right − left). The shorter wall is always the bottleneck — never the taller one.
Start at the widest span; shrink inward. Every shrink trades width for a chance at a taller bottleneck.
Always move the pointer at the shorter wall. Moving the taller pointer is provably suboptimal: same bottleneck, less width.
Each move eliminates an entire set of pairs — all provably ≤ the current area. No optimal pair is ever skipped.
O(n) time, O(1) space. One pass with two variables. This is the expected interview solution.
🎯 When to Use Opposite-Direction Two Pointers
  • Finding an optimal pair where the answer depends on both values and distance
  • A greedy argument exists: moving one pointer direction is provably suboptimal
  • Sorted arrays where a sum is too large/small, letting you confidently skip one end
  • Palindrome checking, pair-sum problems, area maximization
