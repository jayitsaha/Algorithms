LeetCode #2073

Time Needed to Buy Tickets

Everyone queues to buy tickets, buying one per second before cycling to the back. Find the total seconds for person k to finish all their tickets.

Easy Queues Queue Simulation Time: O(n) Space: O(1)
1 Understanding the Problem ▾

Imagine a ticket booth with a single-file queue. Each person wants to buy some number of tickets. The rule: the person at the front buys exactly one ticket per second, then either leaves (if done) or rejoins at the back. You are standing at position k — how many total seconds until you have purchased all tickets[k] of your tickets?

💡 Key Insight

You never need to simulate the actual queue movement. Instead, ask: how many times will each person buy a ticket before person k finishes? That depends on two things: where they are relative to k, and how many tickets they need. Sum all individual contributions in one O(n) pass.

Example: tickets=[2,3,2], k=0

t=1: p0 buys (p0 has 1 left)
t=2: p1 buys (p1 has 2 left)
t=3: p2 buys (p2 has 1 left)
t=4: p0 buys last — done! ans=4

Formula shortcut for k=0

k=0, tickets[k]=2
p0 (i<=k): min(2,2) = 2
p1 (i>k): min(3,1) = 1
p2 (i>k): min(2,1) = 1
Total = 4 ✓

Edge Cases: k is the first person (k=0) — no one is "before" them; k is the last person — no one comes after; everyone wants exactly 1 ticket; person k wants far more tickets than anyone else in the queue.

2 The Algorithm: Direct Contribution Count ▾

Picture the queue cycling in rounds. In each full round, everyone still present buys one ticket. The simulation runs for exactly tickets[k] rounds (the number of times position k appears at the front). Each person's contribution to the total time is simply: how many of those rounds do they participate in?

1 People at positions i ≤ k (at or before k in starting queue): In every round that k gets a turn, i gets a turn first (or in the same round). They participate in min(tickets[i], tickets[k]) rounds — limited by whichever runs out first.
2 People at positions i > k (after k in starting queue): They are behind k and complete their pass in the same round after k. But in k's final round, the simulation ends as soon as k buys — before reaching i. So they only get min(tickets[i], tickets[k] - 1) rounds.
3 Sum all contributions: Each person's contribution is exactly how many times they buy, and each buy is 1 second. Sum = total time.
contribution(i) =
    min(tickets[i], tickets[k]) # if i <= k
    min(tickets[i], tickets[k] - 1) # if i > k
🔑 Why tickets[k] - 1 for people after k?

The queue processes in order: first p0, then p1, …, then pk, then p(k+1), … In the very last round, k buys their final ticket and the clock stops. The people behind k (i > k) haven't gotten their turn in that final round yet — so they get exactly one fewer full pass than k does.

3 Why It Works: The Invariant ▾
🔐 The Invariant

The queue runs for exactly tickets[k] complete passes through position k. In each pass, person i gets one turn if and only if they are still in the queue. Person i leaves after exactly tickets[i] passes through their position. The number of passes through i before or during k's last pass is bounded by both tickets[i] (their supply) and the number of passes through k (the stop condition).

Proving the formula: Arrange the queue starting from p0. In round r (1-indexed), every person still present buys one ticket. Person k participates in rounds 1 through tickets[k]. For person i < k: they appear before k in each round, so they participate in the same rounds k does — capped by their own supply: min(tickets[i], tickets[k]). For person i > k: they appear after k in each round. In round tickets[k], k buys last and stops the queue before reaching i. So person i only participates in rounds 1 through tickets[k] - 1: min(tickets[i], tickets[k] - 1).

⚠️ Common Trap: Applying the Same Formula to Everyone

It's tempting to write min(tickets[i], tickets[k]) for all i. This overcounts for people who come after k. Specifically, when tickets[i] == tickets[k] and i > k, the naive formula gives tickets[k] but the correct answer is tickets[k] - 1. That off-by-one adds up to a wrong total. Always use two branches: one for i ≤ k, one for i > k.

Verification (tickets=[2,3,2,1], k=2): tickets[k]=2. p0: min(2,2)=2. p1: min(3,2)=2. p2 (k): min(2,2)=2. p3: min(1,1)=1. Total=7. Manual simulation: Round1=(p0,p1,p2,p3)=4s, Round2=(p0,p1,p2)=3s, p2 done → stop. 4+3=7 ✓

4 The Code (Annotated) ▾

Solution 1 — Direct Formula (Optimal, Interview Pick)

def timeRequiredToBuy(tickets: list[int], k: int) -> int:
time = 0 ← Accumulate total seconds
for i in range(len(tickets)): ← One pass through every person
if i <= k: ← Before or at k: full passes
time += min(tickets[i], tickets[k]) ← Capped by whoever runs out first
else: ← After k: one fewer pass
time += min(tickets[i], tickets[k]-1) ← Miss the final round (k stops before them)
return time ← Total elapsed seconds
⚠️ Why does i <= k (not i < k) include k itself?

When i == k, the formula gives min(tickets[k], tickets[k]) = tickets[k], which is exactly how many times k buys. No special case needed — including k in the first branch works correctly for free.

Solution 2 — Brute-Force Queue Simulation (Simpler to Derive)

from collections import deque
def timeRequiredToBuy(tickets: list[int], k: int) -> int:
q = deque(range(len(tickets))) ← Queue of indices, not ticket values
time = 0
while True:
i = q.popleft() ← Front person steps up
tickets[i] -= 1 ← Buys one ticket
time += 1 ← One second passes
if tickets[i] == 0 and i == k: ← k just bought their last ticket
return time
if tickets[i] > 0: ← Still needs more: rejoin at back
q.append(i)
💡 Complexity Summary

Direct Formula: Time O(n), Space O(1) — single linear scan.
Queue Simulation: Time O(n × max_tickets), Space O(n) — each buy = one dequeue + possible enqueue.
With n ≤ 100 and tickets[i] ≤ 10⁶, simulation can be 10⁸ ops. Direct formula is 100 ops.

5 🎤 Interview Strategy: How to Arrive at This ▾
Step 1: Recognize the Queue Pattern

Keywords: "line / queue", "one at a time", "rejoin at back", "stop when person k finishes" — this is a classic round-robin queue simulation. The brute-force is always: use a deque, simulate literally. State this first.

Step 2: Spot the Optimization Opportunity

With tickets up to 10⁶, literal simulation is O(n × max). Ask: do I really need to simulate? What changes each round? Only ticket counts. Can I skip ahead? Yes — reason about how many times each person buys before the stop condition fires.

Step 3: Reason About Each Person Independently

Person i's total purchases don't depend on anyone else's behavior — they only depend on where i is relative to k and ticket counts. Draw a small example (tickets=[2,1], k=0) and count manually. You'll see the min() formula emerge naturally.

Step 4: Derive the Off-By-One Carefully

Draw: [_, _, k, _, _]. In the last round, queue processes left-to-right and stops the moment k buys. Everyone at index > k hasn't gone yet in that round. So i > k gets tickets[k]-1 passes, not tickets[k]. Verify with a counter-example: tickets=[1,1], k=0. p1 gets min(1, 0)=0 rounds. Ans=1. ✓

🗣️ What to Say to the Interviewer

"Brute force: use a deque, simulate each purchase one by one, stop when k's count hits zero. That's O(n × max_tickets).

Optimization: I don't need to track the actual queue state. Person i's contribution is independent of others. If i <= k, they get a turn in every round k gets — so they buy min(tickets[i], tickets[k]) times. If i > k, the simulation stops before their turn in the final round — so they buy min(tickets[i], tickets[k]-1) times.

One pass, O(n) time, O(1) space. Sum contributions and return."
🔮 Likely Follow-up Questions
  • "What if two people finish at the same time as k?" → Only people before k in queue matter — those after k still miss the final round.
  • "Time for everyone to finish (not just k)?" → Simply sum(tickets) — no queue logic needed.
  • "Can you do it with a real deque?" → Yes, show the simulation code and explain the trade-off.

7 Summary & Key Takeaways ▾
Queue simulation problems often allow an O(n) direct formula instead of literal O(n × max) simulation.
People before or at k contribute min(tickets[i], tickets[k]) — they share every round k does.
People after k contribute min(tickets[i], tickets[k]-1) — they miss k's final round because the timer stops before their turn.
Including k itself in the first branch (i <= k) handles k's own contribution without a special case.
The key habit: when you see round-robin queues, ask "can I compute contributions independently?" before reaching for a deque.
