Best Time to Buy and Sell Stock II

Deep Dive: State Machine Dynamic Programming

Medium State Machine DP Two States O(n) Time · O(1) Space
1 Understanding the Problem

You're given daily stock prices. You can buy and sell as many times as you want, but you can only hold one share at a time. You must sell before buying again. Goal: maximize profit.

💡 Key Insight

At any moment in time, you're in one of exactly two states:

  • HOLDING — You own a stock (you've bought but not sold)
  • CASH — You don't own any stock (free to buy)

This is what makes it a "state machine" — you're always in one of these two states, and each day you make a decision that might transition you to the other state.

3 Why is This Dynamic Programming?

Dynamic Programming has two key ingredients. This problem has both:

✅ Optimal Substructure

The best solution at day i can be built from the best solution at day i-1. If I know the optimal profit for each state yesterday, I can compute today's optimal.

✅ Overlapping Subproblems

Without DP, we'd recalculate the same scenarios repeatedly. "What's the best profit if I'm holding on day 3?" gets asked many times in a naive recursive approach.

🎯 What we're computing

For each day, we compute:

  • hold = Maximum profit achievable if I END this day holding a stock
  • cash = Maximum profit achievable if I END this day not holding
📐 The Recurrence Relations
hold[i] = max(hold[i-1], cash[i-1] - price[i])
↳ Either keep holding from yesterday, OR buy today (spend price from cash)
cash[i] = max(cash[i-1], hold[i-1] + price[i])
↳ Either stay in cash from yesterday, OR sell today (add price to hold value)
⚠️ Common Confusion: "Why not SELL when updating HOLD?"

The states describe WHERE YOU END UP, not what action you take!

hold[i] asks: "What's the best profit if I END day i HOLDING a stock?"

To END UP holding, I must either:
  • Already be holding → STAY (no action)
  • Be in cash and BUY → transitions me INTO holding

SELLING would move me OUT of hold, not INTO it! That's why sell isn't an option when computing hold[i].

💡 Why subtract price when buying? Why add when selling?

Think of the values as "net profit so far":

  • Buying COSTS money → cash - price (you spend your cash)
  • Selling EARNS money → hold + price (you receive cash)

Example: If cash = 4 (you have $4 profit) and you buy at price = 3, your new hold value is 4 - 3 = 1. You still have $1 of "net position" but you're now holding stock.

4 Understanding the Math: A Concrete Example

Let's trace through Day 3 (price = $3) with concrete numbers to understand why the formulas work.

📊 State at end of Day 2:
hold[2] = -1
Bought at $1, spent $1
cash[2] = 4
Sold at $5, made $4 profit
🔵 Computing hold[3]: "Best profit if I END Day 3 HOLDING"
Option A: Keep holding
hold[2] = -1
I was holding, I stay holding. No change.
Option B: Buy today
cash[2] - price = 4 - 3 = 1
I had $4, I spend $3 to buy. Now I have $1 net + stock.
hold[3] = max(-1, 1) = 1 ← Option B wins!
Why subtract? Buying COSTS money. If I have $4 profit and spend $3, I have $1 left. The -$3 represents money leaving my pocket.
🟢 Computing cash[3]: "Best profit if I END Day 3 NOT holding"
Option A: Stay in cash
cash[2] = 4
I wasn't holding, I stay not holding. Keep my $4.
Option B: Sell today
hold[3] + price = 1 + 3 = 4
I had $1 net + stock. Sell stock for $3. Now $4 cash.
cash[3] = max(4, 4) = 4 ← Tie! Both options equal.
Why add? Selling EARNS money. If my net position is $1 (while holding) and I sell for $3, I receive $4 total. The +$3 represents money entering my pocket.
🧮 Think of values as "net position"

hold = 1 means: "If I sold right now, I'd have $1 profit." It's my current "unrealized" position.

cash = 4 means: "I have $4 in realized profit, no stock held."

5 The Code (Annotated)
# State Machine DP for unlimited transactions
def maxProfit(prices):
# Initialize states for day 0
hold = -prices[0] # If holding on day 0, we bought at prices[0]
cash = 0 # If not holding on day 0, profit is 0
# Process each remaining day
for price in prices[1:]:
# Update hold: keep holding OR buy today (from cash)
hold = max(hold, cash - price)
# Update cash: stay cash OR sell today (from hold)
cash = max(cash, hold + price)
# Answer is cash state (we want to end without holding)
return cash
⚠️ Why return cash, not hold?

At the end, we want maximum profit with no stock in hand. The cash state represents "best profit when not holding" — exactly what we need. Holding stock at the end means unrealized value.

6 🎤 Interview Strategy: How to Think This Up Live

This is the hardest part — how do you derive this solution in a 45-minute interview? Here's the exact mental framework:

📍 Step 1: Recognize the Pattern

Ask yourself: "Am I in one of multiple possible STATES at any point?"

🚦 The Signal: Binary Constraints
  • "You can only hold one stock at a time" → I'm either holding OR not
  • "Must sell before buying again" → Two distinct states with transitions
  • "With cooldown" → Adds a third state (cooldown)
  • "At most K transactions" → States × K
💡 Interview Cue: If you see "at most one" / "can't buy until you sell" / "cooldown period" → think State Machine DP
📝 Step 2: Define Your States Clearly

Write it out explicitly. Say it to the interviewer:

// At the END of each day, I'm in one of these states:
hold[i] = Best profit achievable if I end day i holding stock
cash[i] = Best profit achievable if I end day i NOT holding stock
⚠️ Critical: States describe WHERE YOU END UP, not what action you took. This is the key insight most people miss!
🔄 Step 3: Ask "How Can I Get To Each State?"

For EACH state, list ALL ways to arrive there from the previous day:

"How do I END today HOLDING?"
  • I was holding yesterday, do nothing → hold[i-1]
  • I had cash yesterday, buy today → cash[i-1] - price
"How do I END today NOT holding?"
  • I wasn't holding yesterday, do nothing → cash[i-1]
  • I was holding yesterday, sell today → hold[i-1] + price
🧠 Step 4: Making "hold + price" Click

This is the unintuitive part. Here's the mental model that makes it obvious:

🎯 Think of hold as your "paper profit" while holding
If hold = -2, it means: "I've spent $2 net to get this stock."
If hold = 3, it means: "I've locked in $3 profit AND I'm holding a stock."

It's your REALIZED profit so far + your position.
💰 When you sell: hold + price
Example: hold = -2, today's price = $5
  • I spent $2 to get this stock (that's why hold = -2)
  • I sell it for $5 (I RECEIVE $5)
  • My new cash = -2 + 5 = $3 profit ✓
The insight: hold already has the cost "baked in" as a negative. Adding price gives you the net!
📊 Analogy: Portfolio Balance Sheet
Think of it like a brokerage account statement:
  • cash = Cash Balance (realized money in account)
  • hold = Total Account Value while holding a position
    (could be negative if you just bought and haven't profited yet)
When you sell at price:
new_cash = account_value + sale_proceeds = hold + price
🗣️ What to Say to the Interviewer
"I notice I can only hold one stock at a time, so at any point
I'm in one of two states: holding or not holding."
"Let me define what each state means..."
"hold[i] = best profit if I END day i with stock in hand"
"To end up holding, I either kept holding OR bought today."
"Buying costs money, so that's cash - price."
"To end up with cash, I either stayed cash OR sold today."
"Selling adds money, so that's hold + price."
⚡ Quick Mental Check
Buying? Money leaves → SUBTRACT price
Selling? Money enters → ADD price

8 Summary & Key Takeaways
🎓 Why State Machine DP Works

Instead of thinking about "which days to buy/sell", we think about "what's the best I can do in each state". The DP automatically figures out the optimal sequence of transactions by always picking the better option at each step.

Time: O(n)
One pass through prices
Space: O(1)
Only 2 variables needed
Result: $7
Buy@1→Sell@5, Buy@3→Sell@6
💡 When to Use State Machine DP

This pattern extends beautifully to harder stock problems:

  • With cooldown — Add a third state: "resting" (can't buy)
  • With fee — Subtract fee when selling
  • At most K transactions — Track state per transaction count
