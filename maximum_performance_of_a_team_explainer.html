LeetCode #1383

Maximum Performance of a Team

Pick at most k engineers to maximise: (sum of their speeds) × (minimum of their efficiencies).

Hard Heaps Sort + Min Heap Time: O(n log n) Space: O(k)
1 Understanding the Problem ▾

You have n engineers, each with a speed[i] and an efficiency[i]. You want to assemble a team of at most k engineers to maximise the team's performance score:

Team Performance Formula
performance = (sum of speeds in team) × (min efficiency in team)

The challenge: the minimum efficiency creates a tension. Adding a high-speed but low-efficiency engineer may drag the minimum way down, wiping out your speed gains. The question is: which combination of up to k engineers maximises this product?

💡 Key Insight

In every possible team, exactly one engineer will have the minimum efficiency. If we fix who that bottleneck engineer is, the strategy for the rest of the team is obvious: take the k−1 fastest engineers whose efficiency is at least as high as the bottleneck's. This converts a hard combinatorial search into a clean single-pass scan.

✓ Best Team (k=2, example 1)

Pick E5 (spd=5, eff=7) + E2 (spd=10, eff=4)
sum_speed = 5 + 10 = 15
min_eff = min(7, 4) = 4
performance = 15 × 4 = 60

✗ Poor Team (low min_eff kills it)

Pick E6 (spd=8, eff=2) + E2 (spd=10, eff=4)
sum_speed = 8 + 10 = 18
min_eff = min(2, 4) = 2
performance = 18 × 2 = 36

Edge Cases: k=1 (team of one engineer), k=n (pick everyone), all same efficiency, all same speed. Answer may exceed 32-bit int — use Python's native bigint or apply modulo 10⁹+7 at the end as specified.

2 The Algorithm: Sort Efficiency DESC + Min-Heap of Speeds ▾

We enumerate every possible "minimum efficiency" value by sorting engineers from highest to lowest efficiency. As we visit engineer i, their efficiency is guaranteed to be the smallest we've seen — making them the natural minimum for any team drawn from the processed set.

1 Sort engineers by efficiency descending. Pair each engineer as (eff, spd) and sort. Now engineer at index i has efficiency ≤ all engineers at indices 0..i-1 that we've already processed.
2 Maintain a min-heap of the top-k speeds so far. The heap holds speeds of at most k engineers from those we've seen. Its minimum is always the "weakest link" in our current top-k selection.
3 For each engineer: push their speed onto heap, add to sum_speed. If heap size exceeds k, pop the minimum speed (evict the slowest member) and subtract it from sum_speed.
4 Compute candidate performance: sum_speed × current_eff. Because we sorted by efficiency descending, current engineer's efficiency is the minimum for this group. Update global answer if this is better.
5 Return ans % (10⁹+7). The problem specifies modular arithmetic due to large possible answers.
🔑 Why Min-Heap of Size k Works

We want the k largest speeds among those with efficiency ≥ current minimum. A min-heap capped at size k achieves exactly this: every time we exceed k entries, we pop the smallest speed (the one contributing least to the sum). What remains is always the k fastest candidates seen so far — in O(log k) per operation.

3 Why It Works: The Invariant ▾
🔐 The Invariant

After processing the engineer at sorted index i: the min-heap contains the speeds of at most k engineers from sorted positions 0..i. Their sum is sum_speed. The current engineer at i has the minimum efficiency of all engineers in the heap — because every earlier engineer (positions 0..i-1) had strictly higher efficiency.

Why the optimal team is always evaluated: Consider the globally optimal team T. Let engineer J be the one with the minimum efficiency in T. When our algorithm processes J (in sorted order), it will have already seen all other engineers with efficiency ≥ eff[J]. At that step, the heap holds the best (fastest) k engineers among all seen so far. If T contains engineers with speeds that are in the top-k of their efficiency tier, the heap will hold them — and candidate_performance will equal the optimal performance at that step.

⚠️ "But we evict engineers — do we miss valid teams?"

When engineer X gets evicted from the heap at step i (because a faster engineer joined), it means: for min_eff = eff[i] (or lower for future steps), X's speed contributes less than the new engineer's. The only teams where X would be better are those with min_eff higher than eff[i] — but those were already evaluated in earlier steps, before the eviction happened. Nothing is missed.

Team of fewer than k engineers: The heap can have fewer than k entries if we've processed fewer than k engineers. That's valid — a team of 1 or 2 can be optimal if their performance formula beats any larger combination.

4 The Code (Annotated) ▾

Solution 1 — Sort + Min-Heap (Optimal, O(n log n), Interview Pick)

import heapq ← Python's min-heap (heapq module)
MOD = 10**9 + 7 ← Required by problem statement
def maxPerformance(n, speed, efficiency, k):
eng = sorted(zip(efficiency, speed), reverse=True) ← Sort (eff, spd) pairs by eff DESC
heap = [] ← Min-heap tracking top-k speeds
sum_speed = 0 ← Running total of speeds in heap
ans = 0 ← Best performance seen
for eff, spd in eng: ← Enumerate engineers (eff is cur min)
heapq.heappush(heap, spd) ← Add speed to heap, O(log k)
sum_speed += spd ← Include this speed in running sum
if len(heap) > k: ← Over capacity?
sum_speed -= heapq.heappop(heap) ← Evict smallest speed, O(log k)
ans = max(ans, sum_speed * eff) ← eff is guaranteed minimum; update best
return ans % MOD ← Apply modulo at the very end
⚠️ Critical: Why sort by efficiency, not by speed?

The formula uses minimum efficiency. If we sort by speed, we lose control of what the minimum efficiency is in any given window. Sorting by efficiency descending is the only way to guarantee that engineer i is always the current minimum efficiency — making the formula computable at every step.

⚠️ Apply modulo AFTER computing max, not before

If you apply modulo inside the max() call, comparisons become meaningless (e.g., 10⁹+8 mod 10⁹+7 = 1, which incorrectly appears smaller than 5). Always compute the true maximum first, then apply modulo to the final answer.

Solution 2 — Brute Force (for understanding only, O(n² log n))

def maxPerformance_brute(n, speed, efficiency, k): ← TLE for n=10⁵
ans = 0
for i in range(n): ← Try each engineer as min-eff bottleneck
min_eff = efficiency[i]
eligible = [speed[j] for j in range(n)
if efficiency[j] >= min_eff] ← All with eff >= threshold
eligible.sort(reverse=True)
perf = sum(eligible[:k]) * min_eff ← k fastest × fixed min_eff
ans = max(ans, perf)
return ans % (10**9 + 7)
💡 Complexity Comparison

Optimal (Sort + Min-Heap): O(n log n) sort + O(n log k) heap ops = O(n log n) overall. Space O(k) for heap.
Brute Force: O(n²) iterations × O(n log n) per sort = O(n² log n). Time Limit Exceeded for n=10⁵.

5 🎤 Interview Strategy: How to Arrive at This ▾
Step 1: Spot the Bottleneck Variable

The formula has two parts: sum_speed (you want high) and min_efficiency (you want high). They conflict — adding more people raises sum_speed but risks lowering min_efficiency. Ask: "What if I could lock in what the minimum efficiency is?" This reframes a joint optimization into a parametric search.

Step 2: Fix the Minimum by Sorting

If we sort engineers by efficiency descending, then at step i engineer i's efficiency is always ≤ all previously processed efficiencies. So they naturally become the minimum. This turns "search over all possible min-eff values" into a single left-to-right scan.

Step 3: Greedily Maximize Sum with a Heap

Given min_eff is fixed, we want the k largest speeds from engineers seen so far. A min-heap of size k gives us this in O(log k): push each new speed, pop the minimum if we overflow. The heap always holds the k fastest candidates.

Step 4: Name the Pattern

This is Sort + Min-Heap for Top-K. You sort to control one dimension (min efficiency), then use a capped heap to optimize the other (sum of top-k speeds). Same pattern appears in IPO (#502), Task Scheduling, and Find Maximized Capital.

🗣️ What to Say to the Interviewer

"The tricky part is the min efficiency term in the formula. My idea: fix which engineer provides the minimum efficiency, then greedily pick the fastest others.
I sort by efficiency descending. Now each engineer, when processed, is guaranteed to be the current minimum efficiency. I use a min-heap of size k to maintain the top-k fastest speeds seen so far.
At each step: push this engineer's speed, evict the slowest if heap exceeds k, then compute sum_speed × current_eff and update the answer.
This runs in O(n log n) for sorting and O(n log k) for heap operations, so O(n log n) total. Space is O(k) for the heap."
🔮 Likely Follow-up Questions
  • "What if we want exactly k engineers, not at most k?" → Same algorithm; only evaluate performance once heap reaches exactly size k.
  • "What if the formula used max efficiency instead of min?" → Sort by efficiency ascending; last added sets the max.
  • "Can you do it without a heap?" → Not obviously for O(n log n). A segment tree could work but is far more complex.
  • "Why not sort by speed?" → Then we can't control which efficiency is minimum — we'd have no invariant at each step.

7 Summary & Key Takeaways ▾
Sort by efficiency descending — each engineer naturally becomes the current minimum efficiency as you scan left to right
Min-heap of size k holds the k largest speeds seen so far — O(log k) per push/pop
At each step: candidate = sum_speed × current_eff, update global maximum immediately
Evictions never miss optimal teams — any team involving the evicted engineer was already evaluated at a higher-efficiency step
Apply modulo AFTER finding the true maximum, not inside comparisons
