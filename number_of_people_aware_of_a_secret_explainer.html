LeetCode #2327

Number of People Aware of a Secret

On day 1, one person learns a secret. Each knower waits delay days before sharing and forgets on day forget after learning. How many people know the secret at end of day n?

Medium Queues Queue with Delay/Forget Time: O(n) Space: O(n)
1 Understanding the Problem ▾

On day 1, one person learns a secret. Each person who knows it will:

  • Keep it private for the first delay days after learning
  • Share it with exactly one new person per day for the days after that
  • Forget it entirely on day learned + forget — they are no longer a knower

A person who learned on day L shares on days L+delay, L+delay+1, …, L+forget-1 — a window of forget - delay days. We want the total count of people who still know the secret at the end of day n.

💡 Key Insight

Each person's lifecycle is fully determined by the day they learned. People who learned on the same day behave identically. So instead of tracking individuals, we batch them: dp[L] = count who learned on day L. This collapses an exponentially-growing individual simulation into a simple O(n) recurrence.

2 The Algorithm: Sliding-Window DP on Days ▾

We build a 1D DP array where dp[d] = number of people who first learned the secret on day d. On each day d, the new learners equal the total number of active sharers that day — those who learned on some earlier day L such that L + delay ≤ d ≤ L + forget - 1.

Rearranging that inequality: d - forget + 1 ≤ L ≤ d - delay. So the active sharing pool on day d is the sum of dp[L] for L in that contiguous window — a classic sliding window sum.

1 Initialize: dp[1] = 1 (one person on day 1). Create share = 0, a running sum of active sharers.
2 Loop d = 2 to n: Each day, the window gains a new batch (those who just cleared their delay: dp[d-delay]) and loses an expired batch (those who just forgot: dp[d-forget]). Add the new batch first, remove the expired batch, update dp[d] = share.
3 Final answer: Sum dp[d] only for days d ≥ n - forget + 1 — the people who haven't forgotten by end of day n. Everything before that window has been forgotten.
4 Modulo 10⁹+7 applied at every arithmetic step to prevent overflow. Python's arbitrary-precision integers make this mandatory for correctness on the final return, even though Python won't overflow internally.
🔐 The Recurrence

dp[d] = sum(dp[L] for L in range(d-forget+1, d-delay+1))
Optimized with sliding window: O(1) update per day
share_new = share + dp[d-delay] - dp[d-forget]

3 Why It Works: The Invariant ▾
🔐 The Invariant

After computing each dp[d], the variable share always equals the exact count of people who are actively sharing on day d+1. This is maintained because: we add exactly those who cleared delay on the day they start sharing, and we subtract exactly those who forget on the day they forget.

Why batching is exact (not approximate): All people who learned on day L have identical future behavior — they start sharing on day L+delay, and forget on day L+forget. Since their behavior depends only on L (not on who they are or who told them), they can be grouped into a single count dp[L] without any loss of precision.

Why sliding window works: The sharing window [d-forget+1, d-delay] shifts right by exactly 1 as d increases by 1. Each step adds one new batch to the right end and removes one old batch from the left end. Maintaining a running sum is therefore O(1) per step, giving O(n) total.

⚠️ Off-by-One Alert: Clamping the Window

When d - delay < 1, there's no valid learner to add (that index is before day 1). When d - forget < 1, there's no valid batch to remove. Always guard with if d - delay >= 1 and if d - forget >= 1. The window naturally handles this; Python's slice dp[lo:hi+1] handles it implicitly in the brute-force version.

Final count correctness: dp[d] counts all people who ever learned on day d — whether or not they've forgotten by day n. A person who learned on day L forgets on day L+forget. They still know on day n if L + forget > n, i.e., L > n - forget, i.e., L ≥ n - forget + 1. So sum dp[max(1, n-forget+1) .. n].

4 The Code (Annotated) ▾

Optimal Solution — O(n) sliding-window DP (Interview Pick)

def peopleAwareOfSecret(n, delay, forget):
MOD = 10**9 + 7← prevents overflow on return
dp = [0] * (n + 1) # dp[i] = learners on day i
dp[1] = 1← day 1: exactly 1 person
share = 0 # running active-sharer count
for d in range(2, n + 1):
if d - delay >= 1:← if learners from day d-delay exist
share += dp[d - delay]← they start sharing today
if d - forget >= 1:← if learners from day d-forget exist
share -= dp[d - forget]← they forget today (exit pool)
share %= MOD← keep within mod range (Python % is non-negative)
dp[d] = share← all active sharers teach 1 new person each
lo = max(1, n - forget + 1)← earliest day still not forgotten
return sum(dp[lo:n+1]) % MOD← sum only non-forgetters
⚠️ Why not return sum(dp)?

dp[i] records everyone who ever learned on day i — including those who have since forgotten. We must restrict to days where people haven't forgotten by day n: indices from n - forget + 1 to n. Returning sum(dp) would double-count forgotten people.

Brute-Force O(n²) — for comparison

def peopleAwareOfSecret_brute(n, delay, forget):
MOD = 10**9 + 7
dp = [0] * (n + 1)
dp[1] = 1
for d in range(2, n + 1):
lo = max(1, d - forget + 1)← earliest still-active learner
hi = max(0, d - delay)← latest cleared-delay learner
dp[d] = sum(dp[lo:hi+1]) % MOD← O(n) sum per day → O(n²) total
return sum(dp[max(1,n-forget+1):n+1]) % MOD
💡 The Optimization

The brute-force recomputes overlapping sums: day 5's sum shares most of its terms with day 4's sum. The sliding window keeps a running total share, adding one new term and removing one expired term each step — converting O(n) work per day into O(1).

5 🎤 Interview Strategy: How to Arrive at This ▾
Step 1: Spot the Propagation + Expiry Pattern

The problem has: (a) time-indexed spreading, (b) a delay before spreading starts, (c) expiry after a fixed duration. This is the "queue with delay and forget" shape. You cannot track individuals because the count grows exponentially — you need to aggregate.

Step 2: Define the DP State

"What minimal information do I need to determine the future behavior of a group?" Answer: the day they learned. All people who learned on day L are interchangeable. Let dp[L] = count of people who learned on day L.

Step 3: Write the Recurrence

New learners on day d = sum of dp[L] for L in the active sharing range. Derive the bounds: learner is active if past their delay (L + delay ≤ d) and hasn't forgotten (d < L + forget). Solve for L: d-forget+1 ≤ L ≤ d-delay.

Step 4: Optimize Brute Force → Sliding Window

Mention the O(n²) brute force first to show correctness, then observe that the summation window slides by 1 each step. Maintain a running variable share instead of re-summing. This is a classic "sliding window on DP" optimization.

Step 5: Handle Final Count + Modulo

Emphasize that dp[d] records ever-learners, not current-knowers. Must filter to people who haven't forgotten: dp[n-forget+1..n]. Apply mod at each step for safety.

🗣️ What to Say to the Interviewer

"This is a time-indexed propagation problem with delay and expiry. The key observation is that all people who learn on the same day have identical future behavior, so I'll group them: dp[d] = learners on day d. The new learners each day equal the active sharers — those who learned between day d-forget+1 and d-delay, a sliding window in the dp array. I'll maintain a running sum to update this in O(1) per day. At the end, I sum only dp values where the person hasn't forgotten by day n. Total time O(n), space O(n)."
🔎 Pattern Recognition Signals
  • Spread with delay before activation → delay offset in DP index
  • Participants expire after fixed time → subtract at offset
  • New entries depend on sum of window of previous entries → sliding window DP
  • "Count who are active at end" → distinguish total-ever-learners from current-knowers
  • Values can be astronomically large → mod 10⁹+7

7 Summary & Key Takeaways ▾
Batch people by the day they learned — identical day means identical future behavior
Active sharers form a sliding window [d-forget+1, d-delay] in the dp array — maintain it in O(1) per step
dp[d] = all who ever learned on day d; final answer = only those whose forget day is after day n
Apply mod at every arithmetic step: share can go negative from subtraction, Python's % handles that correctly
Guard window bounds: check d-delay ≥ 1 and d-forget ≥ 1 before indexing
