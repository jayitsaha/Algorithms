Trapping Rain Water

LeetCode #42 — Two Pointers · Opposite Direction (Max Heights)

Hard Two Pointers O(n) Time O(1) Space
1 Understanding the Problem ▼

Given an array of non-negative integers representing an elevation map (each bar has width 1), compute how much water can be trapped after raining.

Example: height = [0,1,0,2,1,0,1,3,2,1,2,1] → 6 units of water trapped.

💡 Key Insight: The Water Level Formula

Water trapped at index i = max(0, min(left_max, right_max) − height[i])

Where left_max = tallest bar at or to the left of i, right_max = tallest bar at or to the right of i.

Water fills up to the shorter of the two surrounding walls. If there's no shorter wall to contain it, water just flows away.

Think of it as a valley between mountains: water can only accumulate up to the height of the lower surrounding peak. The water level at any position is determined by the limiting (shorter) side.

⚠️ Edge Cases to Handle
  • Empty array or fewer than 3 elements → 0 water
  • Monotonically increasing or decreasing → 0 water (no container formed)
  • Flat plateau → 0 water (min(lm, rm) − height = 0)
  • All zeros → 0 water

Approach 1 — Brute Force O(n²)

# For each index, scan left and right to find the tallest bar on each side
def trap_brute(height):
water = 0
for i in range(1, len(height) - 1):
left_max = max(height[:i+1]) # O(n) per position — too slow
right_max = max(height[i:]) # O(n) per position — too slow
water += max(0, min(left_max, right_max) - height[i])
return water

Approach 2 — Prefix/Suffix Arrays O(n) time, O(n) space

# Precompute running max from both directions, then combine in one pass
def trap_dp(height):
n = len(height)
left_max, right_max = [0]*n, [0]*n
left_max[0] = height[0]
right_max[-1] = height[-1]
for i in range(1, n): left_max[i] = max(left_max[i-1], height[i])
for i in range(n-2, -1, -1): right_max[i] = max(right_max[i+1], height[i])
return sum(min(left_max[i], right_max[i]) - height[i] for i in range(n))

Approach 3 — Two Pointers O(n) time, O(1) space ✅ OPTIMAL

Instead of storing two full arrays, maintain running maximums on the fly with converging pointers. The critical observation: when height[left] < height[right], we know the right side already has a tall enough wall (≥ height[right] ≥ left_max), so the water at left is determined entirely by left_max — no need to look further right.

3 Why Two Pointers Works: The Invariant ▼
🔐 The Core Invariant

left_max = maximum height in height[0..left] (inclusive of current left position).

right_max = maximum height in height[right..n-1] (inclusive of current right position).

At any step, if height[left] < height[right], then the bottleneck wall for left is left_max (not right_max), because we're guaranteed a wall on the right side at least as tall as height[right] > left_max. Water at left = left_max − height[left].

This eliminates the need to know the actual right-side maximum: the current right pointer height is sufficient proof that the right wall is tall enough. We never need to backtrack.

1 height[left] < height[right]: Right side guaranteed tall enough. Water at left = left_max − height[left] (if left_max > height[left]). Update left_max if needed. Advance left.
2 height[left] ≥ height[right]: Mirror logic. Left side guaranteed tall enough. Water at right = right_max − height[right]. Update right_max if needed. Advance right.
3 Loop ends when left = right: Every index has been processed exactly once. Total water accumulated is the answer.
💡 Analogy: Filling a Bathtub from Both Ends

Imagine two people sliding towards each other along the tub rim. At each step, the person on the shorter side can calculate how much water is trapped at their position — because they can see the other person is on a higher rim (or equal). The shorter side is always the safe side to process.

4 The Code (Annotated) — Two Pointers ▼
from typing import List
def trap(height: List[int]) -> int:
left, right = 0, len(height) - 1 # Opposite-direction start
left_max = right_max = 0 # Running max from each side
water = 0
while left < right: # Stop when pointers meet
if height[left] < height[right]: # Right side is taller → safe to process left
if height[left] >= left_max: # New tallest bar on left side
left_max = height[left]
else: # Bar shorter than left wall → water trapped
water += left_max - height[left]
left += 1 # Advance left pointer inward
else: # height[left] >= height[right] → process right
if height[right] >= right_max:# New tallest bar on right side
right_max = height[right]
else: # Bar shorter than right wall → water trapped
water += right_max - height[right]
right -= 1 # Advance right pointer inward
return water

Time: O(n) — single pass  |  Space: O(1) — only 4 integer variables

⚠️ Common Mistake: Update Max Before or After Checking Water?

Always update the max before computing water at that step. If height[left] >= left_max, the bar itself is a wall — no water is trapped there, but we extend the wall (update left_max). Only bars below the current max trap water.

⚠️ Why Not Use max(height[left], left_max)?

Equivalent but the if/else form is cleaner and makes the invariant explicit. Both are O(n).
