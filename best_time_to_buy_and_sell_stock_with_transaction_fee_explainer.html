LeetCode #714

Best Time to Buy and Sell Stock with Transaction Fee

Given stock prices and a fee per transaction, find the maximum profit from unlimited buy/sell cycles, paying a fee each time you sell.

Medium Dynamic Programming State Machine DP Time: O(n) Space: O(1)
1 Understanding the Problem ▼

Each day you can decide to buy a stock, sell it, or do nothing. You may hold at most one share at a time, and you must sell before you can buy again. Every sale costs a fixed transaction fee.

Good trade (profit > fee)

Buy at $1, Sell at $8: $7 gain
After fee ($2): net = $5 profit
Worth doing!

Bad trade (profit <= fee)

Buy at $3, Sell at $4: $1 gain
After fee ($2): net = -$1 loss
Skip it!

Key Insight

At every moment you are in exactly one of two states: HOLD (you own a stock) or CASH (you own no stock). The fee raises the bar for when selling is worthwhile. Tracking the best profit achievable in each state — updated each day — gives the global optimum in O(n) time and O(1) space.

Edge cases: single price (no trade possible, return 0), all prices equal (no profit), fee exceeding all price differences (never trade), monotonically decreasing (never trade).

2 The Algorithm: State Machine DP ▼

Model the problem as a two-state finite machine. Each day, both states update from their own best and from a transition:

📦 HOLD Holding stock
SELL (+price − fee)
▶
◀
BUY (− price)
STAY (no action)
💵 CASH No stock held
1 Initialize (Day 0): hold = -prices[0] (bought on day 0, spent that cash); cash = 0 (did nothing).
2 Each subsequent day: Update both states simultaneously from their previous values.
3 Update HOLD: hold = max(hold, cash - price) — keep the existing holding, OR buy today from the cash state.
4 Update CASH: cash = max(cash, hold + price - fee) — stay in cash, OR sell today (from holding) minus the transaction fee.
5 Return cash — maximum profit with no stock held at the end.
The Recurrence Relations
hold[i] = max(hold[i-1], cash[i-1] − prices[i])
cash[i] = max(cash[i-1], hold[i-1] + prices[i] − fee)
3 Why It Works: The Invariant ▼
The Invariant

After processing day i:
• hold = maximum profit if you are holding a stock at end of day i
• cash = maximum profit if you are not holding a stock at end of day i

Optimal Substructure: The best way to be in HOLD at day i comes from exactly two sources: you held on day i-1 (inherited), or you were in CASH on day i-1 and bought today. No other way exists. The max() between these two is optimal.

Overlapping Subproblems: In a naive recursion "what is the best profit in HOLD state at day 5?" would be re-asked from many branches. Here we compute it once per day, O(1) per step.

The fee acts as a natural filter: When you sell at a price only slightly above what you paid, hold + price - fee may be less than the current cash, so the max() chooses to stay in cash. You never need to explicitly check "is this trade profitable" — the recurrence handles it.

Why return cash, not hold? Holding an unsold stock at the end contributes no realized profit. We want maximum realized profit, which is always in the cash state.

4 The Code (Annotated) ▼
def maxProfit(prices, fee):
hold = -prices[0] ⎯ Best profit if HOLDING after day 0 (spent prices[0] to buy)
cash = 0 ⎯ Best profit if NOT HOLDING after day 0 (did nothing)
for price in prices[1:]: ⎯ Iterate days 1 through n-1
hold = max(hold, cash - price) ⎯ Keep holding OR buy from cash today
cash = max(cash, hold + price - fee) ⎯ Stay in cash OR sell today minus fee
return cash ⎯ Max profit with no stock held at end
Update Order Note

In this loop, hold is updated first, then cash uses the already-updated hold. For this specific problem, buying and selling on the same day nets -fee, which is always worse than doing nothing, so the update order is safe. If you want to be explicit, use temporaries: new_h = max(hold, cash-price) then new_c = max(cash, hold+price-fee) then assign both.

Why O(1) Space

Each day's answer depends only on the previous day. We never need the full history — just two scalars. The O(n) table version would have identical results but waste memory.

5 Interview Strategy: How to Arrive at This ▼
Step 1: Recognize the Pattern

Keywords: "unlimited transactions", "at most one share", "fee per transaction", "each day decide". These signal State Machine DP — you have a small set of discrete states and decisions each step.

Step 2: Name the States

Ask: "What information do I need to make tomorrow's best decision?" Answer: only whether I currently hold a stock. Two states: HOLD (profit if holding), CASH (profit if not holding).

Step 3: Write the Transitions

HOLD ← max(HOLD, CASH - price). CASH ← max(CASH, HOLD + price - fee). Each transition asks "what leads to this state today?"

Step 4: Initialize and Return

Day 0: hold = -prices[0], cash = 0. After all days, return cash (no unrealized stock at end).

What to Say to the Interviewer

"At any day I'm in exactly one of two states: holding a stock or in cash.
I track the best profit for each state.
HOLD: max of (stay holding) vs (was in cash, buy today).
CASH: max of (stay in cash) vs (was holding, sell today minus fee).
Init: hold = -prices[0], cash = 0. Iterate. Return cash.
Time O(n), Space O(1)."

When to Use State Machine DP
  • Small fixed set of states at each time step
  • "At most one X at a time" — discrete states with clear transitions
  • Sequential decisions with penalties or constraints
  • Unlimited actions but each has a cost that makes greedy non-trivial
