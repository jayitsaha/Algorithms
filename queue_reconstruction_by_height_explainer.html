LeetCode #406

Queue Reconstruction by Height

Reconstruct a queue so each person [h, k] has exactly k people of height ≥ h in front of them.

Medium Greedy Sort + Insert by K Time: O(n²) Space: O(n)
1 Understanding the Problem ▾

You are given a list of people standing in a shuffled queue. Each person is described as [h, k] where h is their height and k is the number of people in front of them who are at least as tall as them. Your job is to reconstruct the correct ordering.

Think of it like this: you're given people who know their own rank among taller peers, but they've been scrambled. You need to put them back in the right order so everyone's self-reported rank is correct.

💡 Key Insight

If you sort people tallest-first (ties broken by k ascending), then when you insert each person at index k in the result list, exactly k people of height ≥ h are already placed in front. Their constraint is satisfied at the moment of insertion — and inserting shorter people later never invalidates the constraint of taller people already placed.

✗ Shuffled (Invalid) Input

[7,0], [4,4], [7,1],
[5,0], [6,1], [5,2]
(constraints not yet satisfied)

✓ Reconstructed Queue

[5,0], [7,0], [5,2],
[6,1], [4,4], [7,1]
(every k-constraint satisfied)

Verify one entry: [6,1] is at index 3. People in front (indices 0-2): [5,0] (h=5 < 6, doesn't count), [7,0] (h=7 ≥ 6, counts), [5,2] (h=5 < 6, doesn't count). So exactly 1 person of height ≥ 6 is in front. k=1 ✓.

Edge Cases: single person (return as-is), all same height (sort by k, place in order), k=0 for everyone (any descending-height order works), k values that would be impossible (problem guarantees valid input).

2 The Algorithm: Greedy Sort + Insert ▾

The fundamental observation: tall people don't care about short people. If person A has height 7, their k-count only considers people of height ≥ 7. Short people (height 4, 5, 6) are completely invisible to person A. This asymmetry is the key to the greedy strategy.

1 Sort by (-height, k): Tallest first. For equal heights, smaller k comes first. This ensures that when we process equal-height people, the one with k=0 is placed before the one with k=1, so the second can "see" the first in front of it.
2 Initialize empty result list. We build the final queue incrementally. Each insertion maintains the invariant that all placed people have correct k-values.
3 For each person [h, k] (in sorted order), call result.insert(k, [h, k]). At this moment, all existing elements have height ≥ h (because we sorted tallest-first). So inserting at index k means exactly k elements of height ≥ h are in front. Constraint satisfied.
4 Shorter people placed later don't break taller people's constraints. If person T (height 7) is in the result, and we insert person S (height 5) somewhere before T, S has height < 7 and does NOT increment T's k-count. T's constraint remains intact.
[7,0],[4,4],[7,1],[5,0],[6,1],[5,2] → [7,0],[7,1],[6,1],[5,0],[5,2],[4,4]
Sort key: (-h, k). Tallest first. Ties broken by k ascending.
🔐 Why Tallest-First Is Necessary

If we tried shortest-first, when we place a short person at index k, we don't know yet how many tall people will be in front of them after all insertions. The k-count would change as we insert taller people, breaking already-placed constraints. Tallest-first is the only greedy ordering that locks in constraints immediately.

3 Why It Works: The Invariant ▾
🔐 The Invariant

After processing the first i people (in sorted order), every person inserted so far has their k-constraint exactly satisfied. This invariant is maintained because:
(1) All people in result at insertion time have height ≥ current person's height.
(2) Inserting current person at index k places exactly k elements of height ≥ h in front.
(3) Future insertions (shorter people) cannot change any existing person's k-count.

Formal argument for (3): Suppose tall person T (height h_T) is placed at position p with k_T people of height ≥ h_T in front. We later insert short person S (height h_S < h_T) at some position q ≤ p. After insertion, T shifts to position p+1. The people in front of T are now the same people as before, plus S. But S has height h_S < h_T, so S does NOT count toward T's k-count. T's k-count is unchanged. ✓

Equal heights (the subtle case): For people with the same height h, we sort by k ascending. Say we have [h,0] and [h,2]. We insert [h,0] first at index 0. Then when we insert [h,2] at index 2, the elements at indices 0 and 1 include [h,0] and whatever was at index 1 (some person of height ≥ h). Both count toward [h,2]'s k-count since their heights equal h. So k=2 is satisfied. If we had inserted [h,2] before [h,0], the placement would be wrong.

⚠️ Common Mistake: Wrong Sort Direction

Sorting ascending by height (short people first) fails because when you insert a short person at index k, you're counting among the current elements — but taller people haven't been inserted yet. Their future insertions will appear before this person and inflate their actual k-count. You'd have to undo and redo placements. Tallest-first is the only correct greedy order.

4 The Code (Annotated) ▾
def reconstructQueue(people): people = list of [h, k] pairs
people.sort(key=lambda x: (-x[0], x[1])) sort: tallest first, ties by k ascending
result = [] empty queue; we insert one person at a time
for h, k in people: iterate in sorted order (tallest → shortest)
result.insert(k, [h, k]) insert at index k; all existing elements are ≥ h
return result every constraint satisfied by construction
💡 Why result.insert(k, ...) Directly Satisfies k

list.insert(k, val) places val at index k, shifting elements at k and beyond rightward. At the moment of insertion, the result list contains only people of height ≥ h (tallest-first sorting guarantees this). So indices 0 through k-1 all have height ≥ h. That's exactly k people satisfying the constraint. No verification needed — it's correct by construction.

⚠️ O(n²) Due to List Insertion

Python's list.insert(k, val) is O(n) because it must shift all elements after index k one position right. With n insertions, the total is O(n²). For n ≤ 2000 (LeetCode constraint), this is fast enough. To achieve O(n log n), use a Binary Indexed Tree to find the k-th "free slot" in O(log n) each — same greedy logic, faster data structure.

5 🎤 Interview Strategy: How to Arrive at This ▾
Step 1: Recognize the Greedy Structure

The problem asks to reconstruct an ordering where each element's constraint is relative to others. When constraints have a clear "easier to satisfy first" ordering, think Greedy. The k-count depends on how many taller people are in front — taller people's constraints only involve each other.

Step 2: Find the Optimal Sub-Problem Order

Ask: "Who is easiest to place?" Tall people: they only care about equally-tall or taller people. Short people: they care about everyone. So place tall people first — their constraints only involve a small subset (other tall people). Once placed, short people can be slotted in without disturbing the tall ones.

Step 3: Derive the Sort Key

Sort descending by height (primary) so tall people come first. For equal heights, sort ascending by k (secondary) so the person who wants fewer people in front is placed first — enabling the next same-height person to "count" them. Sort key: (-h, k).

Step 4: The Insert-at-k Insight

After sorting, process each person in order. At the moment you process [h, k], every element already in result has height ≥ h. So result.insert(k, [h, k]) directly places this person such that exactly k elements ≥ h are in front. The constraint is met with zero extra verification.

🗣️ What to Say to the Interviewer

"I notice each person's k only counts people of equal or greater height — tall people are 'visible' to shorter people, but not vice versa. This asymmetry suggests a greedy approach: if I handle the tallest people first, I can lock in their constraints immediately. Shorter people inserted later are invisible to them and can't change their k-counts. Sort key is (-h, k): tallest first, ties by smaller k ascending. Then for each person [h,k], just call result.insert(k, [h,k]). Since all existing elements are ≥ h at this point, exactly k of them satisfy the constraint. Done in one pass after the sort. Time complexity is O(n log n) for sort + O(n²) for insertions = O(n²) overall. Space is O(n) for the result. Want me to code it up?"
🔮 Follow-Up Questions

Q: Can we do O(n log n)? — Yes. Use a Binary Indexed Tree to track occupied positions. For each person (sorted tallest-first), find the (k+1)-th free position in O(log n). Same greedy order, faster data structure.

Q: What if two people have [h,k] = [7,0]? — The problem guarantees each [h,k] is unique. But if duplicates were allowed, the algorithm still works — they'd be inserted adjacently.

Q: What if k ≥ n? — Invalid input per problem constraints. The queue has n people so k can be at most n-1.

7 Summary & Key Takeaways ▾
Sort tallest-first so all existing queue elements are always ≥ current person's height at insertion time
Insert at index k directly satisfies the constraint — k elements of height ≥ h are automatically in front
Shorter people inserted later are invisible to taller people — no constraint is ever broken retroactively
Tie-break by k ascending for equal heights — ensures earlier-k people are in front for later-k people to count
Time O(n²) from list insertion; space O(n) for result. Upgradeable to O(n log n) with a Binary Indexed Tree
